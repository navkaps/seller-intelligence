The Product Review Forum is a core mechanism for how Internal Systems aligns early and pressure-tests ideas before work hardens. The forum is intentionally forward-looking and discussion-driven — designed to surface blind spots, sharpen thinking, and strengthen product outcomes, not serve as a status update or readout. Presenters are accountable for a written pre-read at least 48 hours in advance, clear discussion questions, and follow-up on feedback. Reviews focus on who we are building for, the problem being solved, the options and tradeoffs under consideration, and how we are prioritizing.

Starting February 2026, the forum ran 13 consecutive reviews across 8 weeks. What follows is the record of each one.

Feb 3 CPQ Stratus: Launch Milestones and Coverage Plan ›

The first session walked through Stratus's M1-to-M3 coverage roadmap — from 20 deals at launch to 5,500 by M3 — and established the metrics framework that would determine whether the rollout was actually working.

What was at stake

Coverage milestones only mean something if the metrics measuring them are the right ones. The review established a five-dimension framework — adoption, support, performance, friction, and sentiment — as the lens through which M2 and M3 progress would be evaluated. Without this, milestone numbers are counts, not signals.

What was covered

The M1→M3 progression was reviewed: 20 deals at M1, scaling to 5,500 by M3. Each milestone was mapped against expected friction points and the support model needed to sustain coverage at scale. The five-dimension metrics framework was reviewed as the standard lens for tracking rollout health through the rest of the year.

Established

M1→M3 coverage progression locked: 20 deals at M1 to 5,500 by M3. Five-dimension metrics framework adopted — adoption, support, performance, friction, sentiment — as the standard for evaluating milestone health through H1.

Feb 3 CPQ Stratus: SOKRs, Team, and Dec 2026 Roadmap ›

The second Feb 3 session reviewed the full Dec 2026 roadmap for Stratus — establishing what gets built in H1 versus H2, how the team is organized to deliver it, and the SOKR structure that sets the H1 bar.

What was at stake

A roadmap without an explicit H1/H2 sequencing decision leaves teams making local prioritization calls that compound into portfolio misalignment. The SOKR structure — 98% coverage H1, TBP H1, Multi-year H2 — had to be agreed in this room so downstream teams could plan against it. The sequencing of Multi-year into H2 would become a live trade-off question in the Mar 10 review six weeks later.

What was covered

The Dec 2026 roadmap was reviewed against team structure and capacity. H1 SOKRs were set at 98% coverage and Trigger-Based Pricing (TBP). Multi-year deal modeling capability was placed in H2 — a sequencing decision that would be revisited when the Multi-Year Deal Modeling review came back with a case for acceleration.

Decided

SOKR structure set: 98% coverage H1, TBP H1, Multi-year H2. Dec 2026 roadmap approved. Team structure for Stratus confirmed. H1/H2 sequencing locked — this framing set the baseline against which the Mar 10 Multi-Year review later made its trade-off argument.

Feb 10 AI · GTM 2026 GTM AI Strategy ›

The GTM AI strategy review set the 2026 target at 80% adoption across 1,400 GTM people — and mapped the Q1 ship list, the row-level security question, and the action owners who would carry it.

What was at stake

Six AI workstreams were in flight simultaneously: Pricing Quotes in Stratus, Gong expansion, Cross-sell recommendations, Tango+Gong integration, Agentic Outbound, and the Deal Escalation Memo. Without a coordinating strategy, each would ship as a standalone feature. The target was a coherent GTM AI platform reaching 80% adoption across 1,400 GTM people — not a collection of tools.

What was covered

The Q1 ship list was reviewed: Pricing Quotes in Stratus, Gong expansion, Cross-sell recs, Tango+Gong integration, Agentic Outbound pilot launching March 18, and Deal Escalation Memo. Row-level security — the governance question for AI systems accessing deal and merchant data — was put through a RAPID process. Action owners were assigned across Navin, Ilya, Punit, and Shamila.

2026 GTM AI target: 80% adoption across 1,400 GTM people. Q1 ship list confirmed with Agentic Outbound pilot launching March 18. RAPID completed on row-level security. Action owners named across all workstreams — Navin, Ilya, Punit, Shamila.

Feb 10 AI · GTM Tango: Making It the Default Workspace for SAs and ProServ ›

Tango's baseline adoption was 30%. The 2026 goal is 70%. Getting there meant committing to three specific bets — and making Tango the default workspace for Solutions Architects and Professional Services, not just an available tool.

What was at stake

At 30% baseline adoption, Tango was used — but not depended on. The gap between used and depended on is the difference between a tool people touch and a workspace they couldn't work without. Three Big Rocks were the defined path to close that gap: Gong integration to bring conversation intelligence in, Cross-project intelligence to surface patterns across engagements, and User activation observability to understand where adoption was falling off.

What was covered

The 2026 product direction was reviewed against the adoption target: 70% adoption (from 30% baseline), +10,000 hours saved annually, and a 5% reduction in median time from discovery to activation. Each of the three Big Rocks was scoped against these targets. The goal: make Tango the default workspace for SAs and ProServ, not a parallel option.

Decided

Three Big Rocks approved: Gong integration, Cross-project intelligence, User activation observability. 2026 targets: 70% adoption (from 30% baseline), +10,000 hours saved annually, 5% reduction in discovery-to-activation time. Tango positioned as the default workspace for SAs and ProServ.

Mar 3 AI · Infra Domain Intelligence Platform: Build vs. Buy and the Reg Reporting Pilot ›

The Reg Reporting pilot produced the numbers that made the build decision concrete: 3x faster processing, 20% accuracy improvement, 100+ hours saved per month. The harder question in the room was whether to build the platform that makes those results repeatable across domains — or buy.

What was at stake

The Domain Intelligence Platform (DIP) is the infrastructure layer for domain-expert agents across Internal Systems. The buy option — Glean — was evaluated and rejected: $50 per user per month and security concerns around Stripe data made it untenable. The build decision required committing to a Q2 MVP scoped to two domains with knowledge-base-backed agents. The $10M+ estimated impact hung on getting the architecture right the first time.

What was covered

The Reg Reporting pilot results were the anchor: 3x faster, 20% accuracy improvement, 100+ hours per month saved. The build vs. buy analysis was reviewed — Glean rejected on cost ($50/user/month) and security grounds. The Q2 MVP plan was scoped: two domains, KB-backed agents. The $10M+ estimated platform impact was the scale case for investment.

Decided

Build the Domain Intelligence Platform. Glean rejected. Q2 MVP: two domains with KB-backed agents. Reg Reporting pilot — 3x faster, 20% accuracy improvement, 100+ hours/month saved — established as proof of concept. Estimated platform impact: $10M+.

Mar 3 FinSys MaaS Margin Explainability and DNR Strategy ›

MaaS is growing to $1,080M — up 32% year over year. And 10–20% of Issuing margin still can't be explained. That gap, in a business at this scale, is the problem this review was built to surface.

What was at stake

Seven product bridges are needed to explain MaaS margin fully: Payouts, Connect, Capital, Issuing, Treasury, Crypto, and MCS. Without them, Issuing margin — a meaningful portion of a $1,080M business — remains opaque. An additional constraint: 15% of the data isn't updated daily, meaning the numbers finance sees don't reflect current state. The primary business goal — DNR (Do Not Renew) capability by EOY — depends on this explainability infrastructure existing first.

What was covered

The MaaS margin gap was reviewed: 10–20% of Issuing margin currently unexplainable, with 15% of data non-daily. The seven product bridges required to close this were mapped. The resource ask was surfaced: +1 DA for Q2/Q3 and +1 DS. The connection to the BD4 Flash Company SOKR was established — DNR by EOY is a SOKR dependency, not just a backlog item.

Asks surfaced

Seven product bridges scoped across Payouts, Connect, Capital, Issuing, Treasury, Crypto, and MCS. Resource ask: +1 DA (Q2/Q3), +1 DS. Primary goal: DNR capability by EOY — tied to BD4 Flash Company SOKR. MaaS at $1,080M/+32% YoY; margin explainability is a prerequisite for managing it at this scale.

Mar 10 Infra GTM ↔ Product Interlock and the Allocation Engine ›

GTM and Product were looking at the same customer — Lovable Labs — and seeing two different numbers: GTM reported $538K, Product reported $289K. A $249K gap on one account. Across the portfolio: a $226M discrepancy. This review made the case that closing it requires infrastructure, not just coordination.

What was at stake

The $226M difference between GTM and Product F&S views comes from two structural mismatches: GTM uses floating dimensions while Product uses fixed, and the two sides measure different things — AONR (GTM) versus TONR (Product). The gap isn't a data quality error; it's a definitional mismatch baked into how each function plans. Without an allocation engine to reconcile it, ~1,000 hours of manual work per year are spent closing a gap that re-opens every cycle.

What was covered

The Lovable Labs example was the anchor: same customer, same period, $538K vs $289K. The two-phase plan was reviewed: Phase 1 — Anaplan infrastructure (Foundation + Visibility), Phase 2 — Operational Efficiency. Target for the interlock cycle: 3 days → 1 day. Dan Fleming presented as the accountable owner.

Decided

Two-phase plan approved. Phase 1: Anaplan infrastructure (Foundation + Visibility). Phase 2: Operational Efficiency. Target: interlock cycle 3 days → 1 day. Dan Fleming as accountable owner. $226M discrepancy and Lovable Labs example documented as the rationale.

Mar 10 CPQ Multi-Year Deal Modeling Capability ›

1,600 deals last year required multi-year ONR. ~1,200 of them needed L3+ escalations. Every one was modeled outside the CPQ — in spreadsheets — because Stratus couldn't do it. This review asked whether that stays true in 2026.

What was at stake

400+ AEs and 20+ Deal Pricing staff are affected. Each multi-year deal takes 3+ hours of manual effort to model outside the system. The ~1,200 L3+ escalations that required multi-year ONR last year represent deal friction that compounds into deal cycle length and pricing inconsistency. The build-in-Stratus direction had been pre-aligned with Sandeep Menon, Ari Schmorak, and Navin before the review.

What was covered

The targets were laid out: 67% time reduction per deal, >50% of multi-year deals modeled in Stratus within 3 months of launch, 80% below-floor catch rate. Estimated annual savings: ~$2M+. The H1 SOKR had TBP slotted — the sequencing trade-off between Multi-Year acceleration and TBP was the live question in the room.

Decided

Multi-year modeling builds natively in Stratus. Pre-aligned with Sandeep Menon, Ari Schmorak, Navin. Targets: 67% time reduction, >50% modeled in Stratus within 3 months, 80% below-floor catch rate. Estimated annual savings: ~$2M+.

Mar 24 AI · GTM Agentic Outbound: What the Pilot Proved and What Comes Next ›

Since October 2025: ~2,000 hours saved, ~30% higher response rates, ~2x meeting booking rates. Agents now power ~70% of Global MQLs. The pilot launched March 18. This review asked what scale looks like from here.

What was at stake

The 2025 proof points were real. The 2026 question was how to structure the expansion — and whether the org could support more than one outbound motion simultaneously. The end-of-year target: autonomous agents sourcing 12% of all SD outbound SQOs plus 5% from agent-led qualification. Getting there required committing to a three-track architecture: one for Velocity, one for Enterprise, one for inbound qualification.

What was covered

The three tracks were reviewed: Autonomous Outbound (Velocity segment), Agent Co-Pilot (Enterprise segment), and Conversational Qualification. The 2025 numbers served as the baseline: ~2,000 hours saved since October, ~30% higher response rates, ~2x meeting booking rates, ~70% of Global MQLs now agent-powered. The March 18 pilot launch was the live milestone in the room.

Decided

Three-track architecture approved: Autonomous Outbound (Velocity), Agent Co-Pilot (Enterprise), Conversational Qualification. EOY targets: autonomous agents source 12% of all SD outbound SQOs + 5% from agent-led qualification. Pilot live March 18.

Mar 24 AI · GTM Seller Hub: Vision, Strategy, and the Problem It Solves ›

Sellers use Pano, SFDC, Stratus, Gong, and a collection of third-party tools — and none of them supports the end-to-end deal cycle. Sellers spend more time synthesizing across tools than selling. Seller Hub is the answer to that.

What was at stake

The current state: sellers are spending hours scanning accounts, building prioritization in spreadsheets, and losing visibility once deals enter approvals. No single surface supports the full deal cycle from account research through close. The vision had to be compelling enough that sellers would consolidate around it — not add it as another tab. "Seller Hub is where every seller starts their day" — that framing set the bar.

What was covered

The Seller Hub vision was reviewed: the from/to was documented — from scattered context across Pano, SFDC, Stratus, Gong, and third-party tools, to a central surface with AI-driven next actions. The product strategy, early design direction, and how Seller Hub relates to AE Hub and Tango as the underlying intelligence layer were examined and aligned.

Decided

Seller Hub vision approved: "where every seller starts their day." Central surface with AI-driven next actions — not another tool, a consolidation point. Relationship to AE Hub and Tango established. Work to proceed on strategy and early design.

Mar 31 Data Territory Carving: The Groundhog Day Problem and the AAA App ›

~2 million accounts. ~40 Google Sheets. ~20,000 hours of manual work every planning window. And the same data errors appearing year after year — "Groundhog Day." This review asked for 0.5 SWE and a June go-live to end the cycle.

What was at stake

Territory carving happens in a 3-month window, every year, using ~40 Google Sheets to manage ~2 million accounts. The "Groundhog Day" problem — the same data errors recurring annually because there's no system of record — means planning teams spend most of the window on correction rather than strategy. Three options were evaluated: Admin App for AAA only (recommended), Anaplan for AAA, or Admin App for AAA + full carving. The ask: 0.5 SWE through August 2026.

What was covered

The proposal: build an AAA Management App in Stripe Admin with sandboxing capability — the ability to model territory changes before committing them. Target go-live: June 2026, before the next planning window locks. The ~20,000 hours of annual manual work served as the scale case for investment. The three build options were reviewed against cost, timeline, and strategic fit.

Decided

Admin App AAA approved — recommended over Anaplan and full-carving scope. 0.5 SWE through August 2026. Target go-live: June 2026. Sandboxing included: territory changes can be modeled before they commit. Ends the Groundhog Day cycle of same-errors-every-year.

Mar 31 Infra Forecast Hub: Six Tools, 15–20K Hours Lost, No Accuracy Baseline ›

$513.6M in Product Live AONR cannot be forecasted at the product level. Stripe has never had a forecast accuracy baseline. AONR drives 48% of Stripe's growth but only ~10% of in-year TONR. This review made the case for treating forecasting as a product problem.

What was at stake

Forecasting at Stripe is split across six tools: SFDC, P3, PAF, spreadsheets, Pano, and Hubble. None agrees with the others. The result: ~15–20K hours per year lost to reconciliation. No shared source means every function runs its own forecast and resolves the differences after the fact. The fact that no forecast accuracy baseline has ever existed means the problem has never been measured — only felt.

What was covered

The Forecast Hub strategy was reviewed: Gong PoC for Deal Signed forecasting (Go/No-Go decision in Q2), and Activation forecasting built in H2 2026. The $513.6M AONR visibility gap was the central case. AONR's outsized role — 48% of Stripe's growth but only ~10% of in-year TONR — established why product-level forecasting matters even when in-year numbers look manageable.

Decided

Forecast Hub approved as a product investment. Gong PoC: Go/No-Go in Q2 for Deal Signed forecasting. Activation forecasting: build in H2 2026. $513.6M AONR visibility gap and no-baseline-ever condition documented as the rationale. Namrata Rao as owner.

Mar 31 Data Stripe Legal Entity: Source of Truth ›

Eight distinct code paths compute the same legal entity logic — differently. There have been 11+ prior proposals to fix this, none implemented. Two named incidents are in the room as evidence: ir-aspire-word ($61.6M Gibraltar mis-booking) and ir-lizard-seat ($540K uncleared balances). SOX auditors have flagged a Significant Deficiency. This is the review that makes the 12th proposal stick.

What was at stake

Legal entity errors are a projected 8–10 incidents per year, generating >$1B/month in manual corrections by Accounting and $90M/quarter in wrong-entity revenue bookings. The two named incidents anchor the risk: ir-aspire-word ($61.6M Gibraltar mis-booking) and ir-lizard-seat ($540K in uncleared balances). SOX auditors have flagged this as a Significant Deficiency — the highest-severity audit finding before a material weakness.

What was covered

The solution architecture was reviewed: a unified Ruby library in Pay Server and corresponding Scala/Java implementation in Dataland, governed by a SOX-controlled YAML as the single source of truth. Ownership: Reg Platform (MMS). The 11+ prior proposals that never got implemented were the context for why this review needed to produce a different outcome — a named owner, a defined architecture, a commitment to close the SOX finding.

Decided

Solution architecture approved: unified Ruby library (Pay Server) + Scala/Java (Dataland), SOX-controlled YAML. Owned by Reg Platform (MMS). Ashna Jain and Gabriel Lu as accountable owners. Addresses $90M/quarter wrong-entity bookings, >$1B/month in manual corrections, and the SOX Significant Deficiency finding.

What the Reviews Revealed
Five things that kept surfacing — across different teams, different domains

You can plan a review cadence. You can't plan what it surfaces.

We didn't set out to find these themes. They emerged because thirteen different teams, examining thirteen different problems, kept running into the same walls. That's not coincidence — it's signal. And signal is the point.

01
We've been investing in a platform that's working against us.
Stratus appeared in three separate reviews this quarter — and the picture that emerged across all three was the same: Deal Composer's architecture is a ceiling, not a foundation. Not because the team hasn't worked hard. But because the platform itself — Salesforce-bound, governor-limited, brittle by design — extracts more engineering effort than it returns in velocity. A single empty unit test takes minutes. A full suite takes hours. Every new feature is a workaround built on top of a workaround. The reviews gave us the evidence, the forum, and the forcing function to make a call we needed to make.

Four Decisions
The bets made in the room — and what they unlock
Reviews only matter if they generate decisions. These four came directly from the Q1 process.

Stop building for yesterday's CPQ. Accelerate Stratus.

Large-scale (8+ week) Deal Composer investment paused. Engineering resources redeployed to Stratus. 98% deal coverage target: July. Full AE adoption: January 2027.

Why it matters: Eliminates split investment across two platforms. Every engineering hour now compounds on the system that will run Stripe's commercial operations for the next decade.

Multi-year modeling ships on Stratus. Pulled to Q2.

Build on Stratus only — not DC. Accelerated to Q2 by sequencing Trigger Based Pricing to Q3. Unblocks MFC, tiered pricing, and long-term deal structures today handled by spreadsheet.

Why it matters: Enterprise deals that currently require manual multi-year modeling will have a tool-supported workflow by mid-year.

Agentic Outbound: Q1 validated, roadmap accelerated.

First pilot live March 18. Projecting return to SOKR pacing by end-May. Multi-channel expansion (calls, LinkedIn, video) underway. First AI system at Stripe generating attributed outbound pipeline at scale.

Why it matters: This is no longer a hypothesis. The reviews gave it the strategic air cover it needed to move from experiment to investment.

AI agent data access gets governed before it becomes a problem.

Cross-functional alignment reached on merchant-based row-level security for agentic Hubble queries — starting with Gong data. AEs restricted to their own accounts in all AI-driven queries. Approved.

Why it matters: A prerequisite for scaling AI tooling to the entire field without creating data access risk at enterprise scale.

The New Operating Rhythm
Three things Q1 permanently changed

This is the operating rhythm now — not the exception.

How decisions get made

  • Decisions happen in reviews, not after. The most consequential calls of Q1 came directly from the process — not from side conversations or escalations.
  • Pre-reviews are non-negotiable. 100% completion rate this quarter. That preparation showed in every session.
  • Every review enters with a stated point of view and exits with a recorded outcome. No ambiguous results.

What gets strategic attention

  • Infrastructure work is product work. Reviews gave equal air time to Legal Entity Source of Truth and AI Strategy. Both deserved it.
  • Cross-functional alignment is built into the process, not bolted on after. Required attendees mean stakeholders weigh in before decisions, not during escalations.
  • Teams that show up with decision-ready materials signal that they operate at the highest level. That signal is now visible — and it matters.
